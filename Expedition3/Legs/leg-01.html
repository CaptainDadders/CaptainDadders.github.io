All preparations having taken place, including sufficient landing practice for the Norden, I took off from Inter County Airport, Irwin, PA, around 8:00 this morning. Great to finally have the journey underway.

✈ The Journey Begins
Startup, taxi, and takeoff from 31D

I flew straight to the Monongahela River and followed it a short distance to Pittsburgh, and The Point, where three rivers meet: the Monongahela, Allegheny, and the Ohio. This is the spot where Lewis and his crew embarked on their journey.

After spending time reading Undaunted Courage and Lewis's journal entries, I was imagining a wild and natural river with near-wilderness on its banks. But things have changed a bit since 1803. Brunot Island — the site of the infamous air gun incident — now hosts a fossil fuel power plant. Further along I spotted what I thought were 5 cooling towers belonging to the Bruce Mansfield Power Plant. Turns out I was only partly right — 3 of those towers are Bruce Mansfield, which actually closed in 2023, and the other 2 belong to the Beaver Valley Nuclear Power Station right next door. So I flew over both a closed coal plant and an active nuclear facility, all on the same stretch of river Lewis paddled by canoe in 1803.

Bruce Mansfield Power Plant (3 towers, closed 2023) and Beaver Valley Nuclear Power Station (2 towers, still active) — Shippingport, PA

Also spotted on the riverbank was the Mountaineer Casino, Racetrack, and Resort, in Chester, WV.

Mountaineer Casino, Racetrack, and Resort — Chester, WV

Before long it was time to veer slightly off the river and make the approach to 1G8 Eddie Dew Memorial Airpark in beautiful Toronto, OH. The map shows a golf course just left of the grass landing strip, but I didn't see it on the way in — may have been too busy dealing with gusty crosswinds!

Landing was relatively uneventful, and I'm glad to be back on the ground. The trip took me 45 minutes in the air to cover the same distance that took Lewis's expedition almost 6 days!

Pittsburgh to the Ohio: The Journey Begins

Lewis had been supposed to depart Pittsburgh in late July. The keelboat builder missed deadline after deadline, and Lewis's letters to Jefferson during those weeks barely contained his frustration. By the time the boat was finally finished and loaded on August 31, the Ohio River had dropped to its lowest level in four years. The delay had cost him the high water of early summer — and the crew would pay for it every mile down the river.

They pushed off with eleven men, a keelboat, and a pirogue. The very first day nearly ended in disaster. At Brunot Island — just three miles below Pittsburgh, and directly below your flight path — Lewis demonstrated his new air gun for local spectators. The weapon accidentally discharged, sending a ball through a woman's hat forty yards away and cutting her temple. Everyone feared she was dead, but she revived, and Lewis pressed on.

The low water plagued him constantly. The heavily loaded keelboat repeatedly grounded on sandbars and rocky ripples, forcing the crew into the river to push, pull, and drag the vessel through the shallows. Local farmers were enlisted with their horses and oxen to haul the boat over the worst of the rocky shallows. Progress was agonizingly slow — some days barely ten miles.

On September 4, near Georgetown, Pennsylvania, the pirogue carrying the expedition's trade goods sprang a serious leak and nearly sank. Lewis pulled over, spread the wet articles in the sun, oiled everything carefully, and repacked in oilcloth bags. He also purchased a canoe for eleven dollars to help lighten the keelboat's load — only to find almost immediately that it leaked badly and required repairs before it could be trusted. That same day they crossed the state line between Pennsylvania and Virginia, marked visibly by a sixty-foot swath of felled timber stretching across the hills.

September 5 brought fog and rain. The canoes leaked despite repairs. Dark fell with them still unaccounted for — Lewis ordered the trumpet sounded, and they answered from the darkness. They camped that night at Brown's Island, opposite present-day Weirton, West Virginia — just across the river from Toronto, Ohio, where you have just landed.
