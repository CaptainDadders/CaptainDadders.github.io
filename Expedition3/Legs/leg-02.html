Today's flight continued along the Ohio River. Once again, the differences between now and 1803 are quite obvious. In Lewis's expedition, the river above Wheeling was challenging to navigate due to low water. Now, the river has been tamed and has a steady depth. The system of locks assures it is navigable to ships.

On this part of the journey in 1803, squirrels were plentiful and would often be seen swimming in the river. Lewis's dog, Seaman, enjoyed jumping in after them, killing them, and bringing them to Lewis — who would cook them up for himself and the crew. Today, there are far fewer squirrels, and eating anything coming out of the river would be a questionable choice. I continued to pass power plants and smokestacks, and the river is flanked by highways and train tracks.

I passed a couple of spots of historical significance on today's flight. The first such place was Wheeling, WV — which at the time would have been Virginia. Lewis had supplies sent here by ground to keep the keelboat's load as light as possible for the beginning of the journey. The river proved to be much more navigable after Wheeling, so this was a wise decision.

Wheeling, WV — the Ohio River splitting around Wheeling Island

I then flew over the Grave Creek Mound, which Lewis stopped and inspected along his journey. This mound is thought to have been built 2,000 to 3,000 years ago as a burial site. The town that surrounds it today is called Moundsville.

The Grave Creek Mound — Moundsville, WV. Lewis climbed this same mound on September 10, 1803.

Finally, I landed in the Steelton region of New Martinsville, WV at a grass airstrip right on the Ohio. In fact, this airport, P W Johnson Memorial (75D), was decommissioned in 2016 and turned into a park. FS 2024 still considers it a landing strip, however, so I took advantage. The landing was a bit smoother than last time, thanks to the absence of any gusts or crosswinds, but I am still working on my taildragger landing technique.

Wheeling, the Mound, and the Squirrel Migration

The river above Wheeling had punished the expedition with its low water since Pittsburgh — constant groundings, men in the river pushing, horses borrowed from farmers to haul the keelboat over shoals. But by the time Lewis reached Wheeling on September 7, the character of the Ohio began to change. The water deepened, the current steadied, and the worst of the low-water ordeal was behind them.

Wheeling was a genuine waypoint. Lewis had arranged before leaving Pittsburgh for a portion of the expedition's supplies to be shipped overland by wagon rather than loaded onto the already-straining keelboat — and those goods were waiting for him here. He also picked up a temporary passenger: Dr. William Patterson, a young physician from Philadelphia. Patterson's company proved short-lived; he left the party not far downstream, but his presence at Wheeling reflects just how connected Lewis remained to the eastern establishment even as he pushed west.

On September 10, Lewis went ashore near present-day Moundsville, West Virginia, to examine one of the most remarkable earthworks in North America. In his journal he wrote that he “went on shore to view a remarkable artificial mound of earth called by the people in this neighbourhood the Indian grave.” He measured it carefully — ninety feet high, three hundred feet in circumference at the base — and noted the trees growing on its crown. Built by the Adena culture roughly 2,000 to 2,500 years ago as a burial site, the Grave Creek Mound was already ancient and mysterious when Lewis stood atop it. It remains the largest conical burial mound in North America.

The following day, September 11, the expedition entered a stretch of river that Lewis found particularly vivid: a squirrel migration. Gray squirrels were crossing the Ohio in enormous numbers, swimming from the Ohio side toward Virginia. Seaman — Lewis's Newfoundland dog, purchased in Pittsburgh for twenty dollars — proved himself invaluable. He plunged into the river again and again, retrieving the squirrels and bringing them to the boat. Lewis noted that he had Seaman fetch “as many as he had occasion for,” and pronounced them a pleasant food when fried. This hunt took place almost exactly at the area where this leg ends — the stretch of river near present-day New Martinsville — making it one of the closest geographical overlaps between the 1803 expedition and the flight path so far.
