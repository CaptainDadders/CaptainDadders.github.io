Today was my longest flight so far. I had a bit more time to fly tonight, it being the weekend and all, and I have a long way to go, so I figured I'd better make some distance. I took off around 7:30am. In Lewis's journal, he wrote quite a bit about fog coming off the river in the morning. He took air and water temperature readings and concluded that the temperature differential resulted in foggy mornings. I seem to have witnessed this firsthand today. Shortly after taking off, visibility was very compromised and I had to climb above the low clouds and fly at 4,000′ for a while. Thanks to GPS, I was able to still follow the path of the river, even though I could not see it.

Flying through the fog before it got so thick I had to climb above it

Before too long, the clouds cleared and I was able to descend. I was now flying over farmland, which was a nice change of scenery from the rather industrial setting for much of the earlier journey.

A rural stretch of the Ohio River

Having said that, I eventually came across yet more power plants. Apparently, the Ohio River is a great place to put power plants because of the abundant cooling water. Additionally, it is close to coal supplies, as well as power-hungry populations. I have passed both coal and nuclear plants, although I must say it is hard to tell the difference.

Yet another power plant along the Ohio River

I reached my landing spot, Robert Newlon Field (I41), near Huntington, WV, with not much fuel to spare. My landing technique in this plane still needs work. I seem to be quite prone to ground looping after landing. Fortunately, I was able to stop without damaging the plane or putting it into the river!

Science on the River: Fog, Temperature, and the Long Drift South

The stretch of the Ohio between New Martinsville and the Huntington area was one of the more monotonous sections of the outbound journey — long, winding river miles with few dramatic incidents. But Lewis was never simply drifting. He filled these days with the systematic scientific observations that Jefferson had commissioned, recording air and water temperatures each morning and evening, noting weather conditions, sketching wildlife, and documenting anything that struck him as new or significant.

The river fog that obscured the flight path for this leg was a phenomenon Lewis observed and recorded carefully. He measured the temperature of the air against the temperature of the river water and noted that the warmer river surface, cooling more slowly than the air overnight, produced a dense morning mist that could hang over the Ohio for hours. His methodology was simple but disciplined: he kept a thermometer at the bow and took readings at the same times each day. These were not casual observations — Jefferson had tasked him with cataloguing the natural world along the entire route, and Lewis took that responsibility seriously.

On September 13, the expedition reached Marietta, Ohio, at the confluence of the Muskingum River. Marietta was among the oldest permanent settlements in the Northwest Territory, founded in 1788, and it was a genuine town — a marked contrast to the wilderness stretches upstream. Here, Lewis collected correspondence that had been forwarded from Washington, caught up on mail, and briefly enjoyed the company of people connected to the wider world. The stop was short; the river and the mission pressed him onward.

Further along, the Ohio passed Blennerhassett Island, near present-day Belpre, Ohio, home of the wealthy Irish immigrant Harman Blennerhassett. At the time Lewis passed, the island estate was one of the most lavish private properties in the western country. Within a few years it would be swept up in the Aaron Burr conspiracy, and Blennerhassett himself would be ruined — but in September 1803, from the water, it was simply a remarkable house on a beautiful island.

By around September 17, the expedition approached Point Pleasant, West Virginia, where the Great Kanawha River enters the Ohio from the east. The Kanawha was a significant landmark — Lewis noted it in his journal — and the country around Point Pleasant had its own history, having been the site of a major battle between Virginia militia and Shawnee warriors in 1774. The flight path for this leg ends just downstream of this confluence, near the area where Huntington, West Virginia now stands. Lewis and the crew pressed on, still carrying the river's current toward the Mississippi.
