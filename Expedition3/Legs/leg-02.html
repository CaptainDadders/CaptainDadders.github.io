Today's flight continued along the Ohio River. Once again, the differences between now and 1803 are quite obvious. In Lewis's expedition, the river above Wheeling was challenging to navigate due to low water. Now, the river has been tamed and has a steady depth. The system of locks assures it is navigable to ships.

On this part of the journey in 1803, squirrels were plentiful and would often be seen swimming in the river. Lewis's dog, Seaman, enjoyed jumping in after them, killing them, and bringing them to Lewis — who would cook them up for himself and the crew. Today, there are far fewer squirrels, and eating anything coming out of the river would be a questionable choice. I continued to pass power plants and smokestacks, and the river is flanked by highways and train tracks.

I passed a couple of spots of historical significance on today's flight. The first such place was Wheeling, WV — which at the time would have been Virginia. Lewis had supplies sent here by ground to keep the keelboat's load as light as possible for the beginning of the journey. The river proved to be much more navigable after Wheeling, so this was a wise decision.

Wheeling, WV — the Ohio River splitting around Wheeling Island

I then flew over the Grave Creek Mound, which Lewis stopped and inspected along his journey. This mound is thought to have been built 2,000 to 3,000 years ago as a burial site. The town that surrounds it today is called Moundsville.

The Grave Creek Mound — Moundsville, WV. Lewis climbed this same mound on September 10, 1803.

Finally, I landed in the Steelton region of New Martinsville, WV at a grass airstrip right on the Ohio. In fact, this airport, P W Johnson Memorial (75D), was decommissioned in 2016 and turned into a park. FS 2024 still considers it a landing strip, however, so I took advantage. The landing was a bit smoother than last time, thanks to the absence of any gusts or crosswinds, but I am still working on my taildragger landing technique.

From Wheeling to the Long Reach

The keelboat reached Wheeling on September 7, 1803, after another stretch of grinding work over the shallows. For Lewis, Wheeling was a planned waypoint, not a chance stop. He had arranged for additional supplies to be sent overland from Pittsburgh by wagon — goods too heavy to risk on the keelboat in the low water above. From Wheeling onward, the Ohio ran deeper and more reliable; lightening the boat for the upper river had been the right gamble. He stayed two days to repack, take on the cached supplies, and rest the men.

Below Wheeling the river finally cooperated. On September 10 the boats came abreast of a place the locals called the Indian grave. Lewis went ashore with a few of the men and walked the seven hundred paces inland from the bank to see it for himself. He recorded its dimensions carefully — height, base, the ditch around it, and the great white oak growing from the summit, whose girth he measured at thirteen and a half feet. He guessed the tree at three hundred years old, and reasoned that whatever people had built the mound, they had done so long before that. The earthwork that captured his attention is now known to be Adena, raised between roughly 250 and 150 BC, and it remains the largest conical burial mound in North America.

"I . . . went on shore to view a remarkable artificial mound of earth called by the people in this neighbourhood the Indian grave." — Meriwether Lewis, Journal, September 10, 1803

The day after the mound — September 11 — brought one of the more peculiar entries in the early journals. The river was alive with squirrels, hundreds of them, swimming the Ohio in a mass migration that Lewis observed with naturalist's curiosity. Seaman, his Newfoundland, took the matter as a personal commission. The dog leapt in repeatedly, retrieving squirrel after squirrel and carrying them dripping back to the boat. Lewis fried them and pronounced the result a pleasant food. The flight path for this leg ends just south of where that strange day on the water unfolded, in the country the boatmen of the era called the Long Reach.
